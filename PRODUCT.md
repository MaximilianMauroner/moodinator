# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Moodinator is for people tracking their emotional wellbeing privately, often through several quick check-ins across a day. They need low-friction capture in the moment, enough detail when they have time, and a way to review patterns without feeling judged or pushed. Some users will export their history for a therapist, so the product must treat entries as sensitive personal records rather than casual activity data.

## Product Purpose

Moodinator helps people log moods, emotions, context, energy, notes, photos, and voice memos locally on their own device. It exists to support self-awareness through repeated check-ins, trend discovery, and therapist-ready exports while preserving the promise that private emotional data stays under the user's control. Success means users can capture how they feel quickly, return later to understand patterns, and share a clear export when they choose.

## Brand Personality

Calm, trustworthy, grounded. The product voice should feel plainspoken and respectful: supportive without coaching too hard, clear without becoming clinical, and warm without turning mood tracking into a game. Interfaces should feel like a private personal tool, not a health institution or a growth-hacking app.

## Anti-references

Avoid clinical or hospital-like UI, generic AI-dashboard aesthetics, forced cheerfulness, surgical sterility, and anything that suggests data extraction. Do not make the app feel like it is harvesting, scoring, diagnosing, or monetizing the user's emotional state. Avoid wellness gimmicks that pressure users to maintain streaks at the expense of honest tracking.

## Design Principles

1. Privacy is the product promise. Every data-related flow should reinforce local control, consent, and reversibility.
2. Logging must stay lightweight. Quick check-ins should be reachable without turning every entry into a form.
3. Depth should be optional. Detailed entries, media, context tags, and therapist exports should be available without burdening everyday use.
4. Insights should reduce ambiguity, not judge the user. Trends, patterns, and charts should help users notice what changed while avoiding diagnostic language.
5. Emotional data deserves calm handling. Visual emphasis, copy, and motion should avoid alarmist framing unless the user explicitly enters an urgent state.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA contrast and preserve clear screen-reader labels for mood values, charts, settings, and entry actions. Mood information should not rely on color alone because the scale is central to the app and the mood direction is inverted: lower numbers are better. Support reduced motion where animations are not essential, keep touch targets comfortable for repeated mobile use, and write labels that are understandable outside a clinical or therapy context.
